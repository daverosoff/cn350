    Subsection 1.1.1 Class Activities

    Activity 1.1.1.

    Negate the following statement:
    \(\text{Mike and Karen are tall.}\)
    Choose the correct statement:
    • Mike is not tall, or Karen is not tall
    • Mike is tall, or Karen is tall
    • Mike is not tall, and Karen is not tall
    • Mike is tall, and Karen is tall
    Solution.
    \(\text{Mike and Karen are tall.}\) Let \(p\) be the statement \(\text{Mike is tall}\) Let \(q\) be the statement \(\text{Karen is tall}\) First, translate the statement into formal logic. In this statement, \(p\) is true, and \(q\) is true. Therefore, the correct answer is \(p\) and \(q\text{,}\) or \(p\wedge q\text{.}\) Now, negate the statement. \(\mathbin{\sim} (p\wedge q)\) \(\equiv ( \mathbin{\sim} p\vee \mathbin{\sim} q)\) Thus, the answer is \(\mathbin{\sim} p\vee \mathbin{\sim} q\) Recall what \(p\) and \(q\) represent: \(\text{p: Mike is tall}\) \(\text{q: Karen is tall}\) From \(\mathbin{\sim} p\vee \mathbin{\sim} q\text{,}\) we get \(\text{Mike is not tall, or Karen is not tall.}\)

    Activity 1.1.2.

    Buttercup knows whether or not Westley is lying. She promises that if Westley is lying, she will give you a cookie. Buttercup always keeps her promises.
    Suppose she does not give you a cookie; what can you conclude?
    • Westley is lying.
    • Westley is not lying.
    • Not enough information to determine.
    Suppose she gives you a cookie; what can you conclude?
    • Westley is lying.
    • Westley is not lying.
    • Not enough information to determine.
    Answer 1.
    \(\text{Westley is not lying.}\)
    Answer 2.
    \(\text{Not enough information to determine.}\)
    Solution.

    Activity 1.1.3.

    Negate the following statement:
    \(\text{If Mary fails her classes, then she cannot graduate.}\)
    \(\text{p: Mary fails her classes}\)
    \(\text{q: Mary can graduate}\)
    Write the statement in formal logic:
    • \(\displaystyle \mathbin{\sim} p\rightarrow q\)
    • \(\displaystyle q\rightarrow p\)
    • \(\displaystyle p\rightarrow \mathbin{\sim} q\)
    • \(\displaystyle p\rightarrow q\)
    Negate the logic:
    • \(\displaystyle \mathbin{\sim} p\wedge \mathbin{\sim} q\)
    • \(\displaystyle \mathbin{\sim} p\wedge q\)
    • \(\displaystyle p\wedge q\)
    • \(\displaystyle \mathbin{\sim} p\vee \mathbin{\sim} q\)
    Rewrite the negated logic in English
    • Mary does not fail her classes or she cannot graduate
    • Mary does not fail her classes and she cannot graduate
    • Mary does not fail her classes and she can graduate
    • Mary fails her classes and she can graduate
    Solution.
    \(\text{If Mary fails her classes, then she cannot graduate.}\) \(\text{p: Mary fails her classes}\) \(\text{q: Mary can graduate}\) In this statement, if \(p\) is true, then \(q\) is false. This is an \(implies\) relationship. Thus, the answer is \(p\rightarrow \mathbin{\sim} q\) Now, negate the statement. In order to negate this statement, first translate it into an \(or\) statement to get rid of the \(implies\) operator. \(p\rightarrow \mathbin{\sim} q\equiv \mathbin{\sim} p\vee \mathbin{\sim} q\) \(\mathbin{\sim} ( \mathbin{\sim} p\vee \mathbin{\sim} q)\) \(\equiv p\wedge q\) Thus, the answer is \(p\wedge q\) Recall what \(p\) and \(q\) represent: \(\text{p: Mary fails her classes}\) \(\text{q: Mary can graduate}\) From \(p\wedge q\text{,}\) we get \(\text{Mary fails her classes, and she can graduate.}\)

    Activity 1.1.4.

    Negate the following statement.
    \(\text{Billy and Bob are applying for the same job, but only one can succeed.}\)
    \(\text{p: Billy gets the job}\)
    \(\text{q: Bob gets the job}\)
    Choose the correct statement:
    • \(\displaystyle \mathbin{\sim} (p\wedge q)\)
    • \(\displaystyle \mathbin{\sim} (p\vee q)\)
    • \(\displaystyle p\vee q\)
    • \(\displaystyle p\wedge q\)
    Solution.
    \(\text{Billy and Bob are applying for the same job, but only one can succeed.}\) \(\text{p: Billy gets the job}\) \(\text{q: Bob gets the job}\) First, translate the statement into formal logic. In this statement, \(p\) can be true, xor \(q\) can be true, or neither can be true. The key here is that while either one can succeed, there is no guarantee of success. The expression is then equal to \(\mathbin{\sim} (p\wedge q)\) Now, negate the statement. \(\mathbin{\sim} ( \mathbin{\sim} (p\wedge q))\) \(\equiv \mathbin{\sim} \mathbin{\sim} (p\wedge q)\) \(\equiv p\wedge q\) The answer is then \(p\wedge q\) As an extra exercise, what would this statement translate to in English?

    Activity 1.1.7.

    We say a set \(A\) is finite if there is a nonnegative integer \(n\) such that the proposition
    \begin{equation} A \text{ has $n$ elements}\tag{1.1.1} \end{equation}
    is true. If \(A\) is a finite set, then \(\abs{A}\) denotes the size of \(A\text{,}\) the number of its elements.
    A one-to-one correspondence or bijection is a function that can be reversed. Given a function \(f \colon A \to B\text{,}\) you can tell that \(f\) is a bijection if it pairs each “input” (argument) to a unique output (value).
    (a)
    Let \(A\) be a finite set. How many functions are there with domain \(A\) and codomain \(\mathbf{\overline{2}} = \{0,1\}\text{?}\)
    Hint.
    Try listing all possibilities for \(\abs{A} = 1, 2, 3\text{.}\) A pattern should emerge. Once you think you see it, try to explain why that is the answer. The lists you made for the small examples should help you see the “general” case.
    (b)
    Establish a one-to-one correspondence (i.e., a bijection) between the set of functions \(A \to \mathbf{\overline{2}}\) and the power set \(2^A = \{ B : B \subseteq A \}\text{.}\)
    Hint.
    The “inputs” of your bijection could be the functions \(A \to \mathbf{\overline{2}}\) and the “outputs” the elements of the set \(2^A\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    Define a function
    \begin{equation*} \mathrm{ev}_1 \colon \{ f : f \text{ is a function $A \to \mathbf{\overline{2}}$} \} \to 2^A \end{equation*}
    by the equation \(\mathrm{ev}_1(f) = \{ x \in A : f(x) = 1 \}\text{.}\) We will show that \(\mathrm{ev}_1\) is a bijection. Bijections are functions with two properties:
    1. If \(s \ne t\) then \(G(s) \ne G(t)\) (no overlap/collision; at most one inbound arrow for each codomain element)
    2. For each \(y\) in the codomain, there is \(x\) in the domain such that \(G(x) = y\) (every codomain element “covered”; at least one inbound arrow for each codomain element)
    Suppose that \(f\) and \(g\) are distinct functions \(A \to \mathbf{\overline{2}}\text{.}\) We need to show that \(\mathrm{ev}_1(f) \ne \mathrm{ev}_1(g)\text{.}\) The first is the subset of \(A\) on which \(f\) takes the value \(1\text{.}\) The second is the same set, but for \(g\text{.}\) Since \(f \ne g\text{,}\) there is some \(x \in A\) where \(f(x) \ne g(x)\text{.}\) Since the only possible values of these functions are \(0\) and \(1\text{,}\) it follows that exactly one of \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\) is equal to \(1\text{.}\) Hence \(x\) is a member of \(\mathrm{ev}_1(f)\) or \(\mathrm{ev}_1(g)\text{,}\) but not both. This shows that \(\mathrm{ev}_1\) has the first property of a bijection.
    To obtain the second, let us choose an arbitrary element \(S \in 2^A\text{.}\) This \(S\) is just a subset of \(A\text{.}\) We must show that there exists a function \(h\) such that \(\mathrm{ev}_1(h) = S\text{.}\) We may of course define
    \begin{equation*} h(x) = \begin{cases} 0 \amp \text{if $x \notin S$} \\ 1 \amp \text{if $x \in S$} \\ \end{cases} \end{equation*}
    and then we see at once that \(h\) has the desired property. We have now shown that \(\mathrm{ev}_1\) has both properties of a bijection.
    (c)
    Conclude that \(\abs{\{f \colon A \to \mathbf{\overline{2}}\}} = \abs{2^A}\text{,}\) and identify the common value of these expressions. (Your answer will depend on \(A\text{.}\))
    Solution.
    Since we have seen a bijection between these sets, they have the same size. We listed all the binary functions on \(n\) variables; there are \(2^n\) of them, so there must be \(2^n\) subsets of \(A\) as well. Observe these combine to say
    \begin{equation*} \abs{2^A} = 2^{\abs{A}} \end{equation*}
    for every finite set \(A\text{.}\)
