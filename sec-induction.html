    Section 1.2 Induction

    Objectives

    Theorem 1.2.1.

    Let \(A\) be a subset of the natural numbers \(\N\) satisfying
    1. \(0 \in A\text{;}\) and
    2. If \(n \in A\text{,}\) then \(n+1 \in A\text{.}\)
    Then \(A = \N\text{.}\)

    Remark 1.2.2.

    We usually apply the induction theorem in a highly implicit way. It is only invoked by name in introductory texts like this one. We have a sequence of theorems we wish to prove by induction. Usually we are too lazy to speak this way and we say we are proving one theorem, but about every natural number instead of a specific one (or about trees of arbitrary height instead of trees of height \(n\)). Each theorem in our sequence has a hypothesis \(P_n\) and a conclusion \(Q_n\text{.}\) Often all of the \(P_n\) are the same, but it doesn‚Äôt hurt anything to let them be different. It happens even more often that all of the \(Q_n\) are specializations of a statement about integers to a specific integer. What we do when we write a proof by induction is to apply the induction theorem to the set \(S\) defined by
    \begin{equation*} S = \{ n \in \N : P_n \implies Q_n \}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    that is, the set of natural numbers (or e.g. tree heights) for which our theorem holds. If we can show the hypotheses of the induction theorem apply to the set \(S\text{,}\) our proof is complete, because the conclusion of the induction theorem then entails that \(S = \N\text{.}\)
    This is what I meant in class when I spoke of an ‚Äúargument machine‚Äù. As stated above, none of this framework is ever explicitly mentioned in practice. In undergraduate books, it is considered enough to mention induction, prove a ‚Äúbase case‚Äù and an ‚Äúinduction step‚Äù or ‚Äúinductive case‚Äù (two phrases for the same thing), and voil√†! The proof is complete.
    Thus, the ‚Äúinduction framework‚Äù consists of arranging your argument so that the sequences \(P_n\) and \(Q_n\) are clear. We call the theorem ‚Äú\(P_0 \implies Q_0\)‚Äù the ‚Äúbase case‚Äù. This theorem could also be stated as ‚Äú\(0 \in S\)‚Äù.
    The inductive case is always phrased as a conditional. ‚ÄúIf \(n\) is a natural number that is in \(S\text{,}\) then \(n+1\) is also in \(S\text{.}\)‚Äù If you recall the definition of \(S\text{,}\) you will see that this conditional is equivalent to
    \begin{equation*} \left( P_n \implies Q_n \right) \implies \left( P_{n+1} \implies Q_{n+1} \right)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    So, we usually formulate our induction step in the latter way. My induction hypothesis would be ‚Äú\(P_n\) implies \(Q_n\)‚Äù. From this hypothesis I would attempt to deduce the conclusion, ‚Äú\(P_{n+1}\) implies \(Q_{n+1}\)‚Äù.
    As a final thought, I should tell you that in our real writing, we don‚Äôt usually assign values like \(P_n\) to specific predicates like ‚Äú\(6\) divides \(n^3 - n\)‚Äù. I have done so here to aid in my clear expression, but you should try to craft your argument without using phrases like:
    1. ‚Äúnow let \(k = n\)‚Äù
    2. ‚Äúassume \(P_n\)‚Äù
    3. ‚Äúthe theorem is true for \(n\)‚Äù

    Subsection 1.2.1 Class activities

    Activity 1.2.1.

    (a)
    Show, using induction, that if \(n\) is a natural number, then \(3\) divides \(4^n - 1\text{.}\)
    Here \(P_n\) is the empty statement and \(Q_n\) is the statement ‚Äú\(3\) divides \(4^n - 1\)‚Äù. The empty statement is indistinguishable from the logical constant \(\textsf{True}\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    The base case is to show that \(0\) satisfies the conclusion of the statement. But \(3 \cdot 0 = 0 = 4^0 - 1\text{,}\) so the base case is done.
    The inductive case is always phrased as a conditional. ‚ÄúIf \(n\) is a natural number that is in \(A\text{,}\) then \(n+1\) is also in \(A\text{.}\)‚Äù Let us prove this statement. We will use a direct proof, assuming the hypothesis (\(3\) divides \(4^n - 1\)) and deducing the conclusion (\(3\) divides \(4^{n+1} - 1\)).
    Since \(3\) divides \(4^n - 1\text{,}\) the definition of divisibility tells us there is \(k\) such that
    \begin{equation*} 3k = 4^n - 1. \end{equation*}
    Multiplying this equation by 4 and adding 3, we obtain
    \begin{equation*} 12k + 3 = 4(4^n - 1) + 3 = 4^{n+1} - 4 + 3 = 4^{n+1} - 1. \end{equation*}
    Since \(12k + 3 = 3(4k + 1)\text{,}\) we have shown that \(3 \cdot (4k + 1) = 4^{n+1} - 1\) and the induction step is complete, as is the proof.

    Activity 1.2.2.

    Prove that for all natural numbers \(n\text{,}\) \(6\) divides \(n^3 - n\text{.}\) Use the induction framework.
    Hint.
    The base case is \(n = 0\) just like before. The inductive step is to prove: if \(6\) divides \(n^3 - n\text{,}\) it also divides \((n+1)^3 - (n+1)\text{.}\)
    Here are some helpful facts that you can use without proof. In a math class, we‚Äôd prove these as exercises as well, but here I‚Äôm hoping to provide enough math hints that you can focus more on the logical structure of the argument and less on the arithmetic.
    1. If \(3\) divides \(\ell\) and \(2\) divides \(\ell\text{,}\) then \(6\) divides \(\ell\text{.}\)
    2. ‚Äú\(a\) is even‚Äù is the same statement as ‚Äú\(2\) divides \(a\)‚Äù.
    3. If \(a\) divides \(b\text{,}\) then \(a\) divides \(kb\) for all integers \(k\text{.}\)
    4. If \(a\) divides \(b\) and \(a\) divides \(c\text{,}\) then \(a\) divides \(b \pm c\text{.}\)

    Activity 1.2.3.

    In this activity we deal with points in the \(n\)-dimensional Euclidean space \(\R^n\text{.}\) Such a point is specified by an \(n\)-tuple of coordinates \((x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n)\text{,}\) where each \(x_i \in \R\text{.}\)
    The unit cube in \(\R^n\text{,}\) or unit \(n\)-cube, is the subset \(I^n\) of \(\R^n\) defined by
    \begin{equation*} I^n = \{ (x_1, \ldots, x_n) \colon 0 \leq x_i \leq 1 \text{ for all $i$} \}. \end{equation*}
    Just as a 3-dimensional cube has 2-, 1-, and 0-dimensional faces (usually called faces, edges, and corners, respectively), the \(n\)-cube has faces of all lower dimensions. We are interested in the corners.
    The corners of \(I^n\) are defined to be the points of \(I^n\text{,}\) all of whose coordinates are either 0 or 1. For example, \((0, 1, 1, 1, 0, 1, 0)\) is a corner of \(I^7\text{.}\)
    (a)
    Prove that \(I^n\) has \(2^n\) corners using the induction framework.

    Activity 1.2.4.

    (a)
    Consider a rectangular \(2 \times n\) array of squares, and the problem of determining the number of ways it may be tiled by \(n\) dominoes. Each domino must cover exactly 2 adjacent squares, without overlaps. So, one way is to use all parallel dominoes. If \(n\) happens to be even, all horizontal dominoes will work as well.
    Of course, other tilings are also possible.
    You can probably draw all 13 tilings of the \(2 \times 6\) grid quite easily. It turns out that 13 is also the value of \(F_7\text{,}\) the 7th Fibonacci number. This isn‚Äôt a coincidence, as you‚Äôre asked to show below.
    Using the recurrence relation above and the induction framework, show that there are \(F_{n+1}\) such tilings of the \(2 \times n\) array by dominoes, for all \(n \geq 0\text{.}\)
    Note. For \(n = 0\text{,}\) we say that there is one way to tile an empty array with no dominoes. There would be zero ways to do it with more dominoes, and zero ways to tile a nonempty array with no dominoes.
    Hint.
    Consider the ways in which a tiling of a \(2 \times (n + 2)\) array can arise from a smaller tiling. Remember, you need to show that the number of \((n+2)\)-tilings is the sum of the number of \(n\)-tilings and the number of \((n+1)\)-tilings.
    Answer.
    If we look at the last column of an \((n+2)\)-tiling, it must be either a single vertical domino or the two ends of two horizontal ones. If the former, removing it yields an \((n+1)\)-tiling. If the latter, removing both dominoes yields an \(n\)-tiling.
    Solution.
    We will use the induction framework to prove the following claim: For each \(n \in \N\text{,}\) the number of tilings of an \(n \times 2\) grid by dominoes is equal to \(F_{n+1}\text{.}\) Recall first the recurrence relation for the Fibonacci numbers:
    \begin{align*} F_1 \amp = 1\\ F_2 \amp = 1\\ F_{n+1} \amp = F_n + F_{n-1} \quad \text{for all $n \in \N$}\text{.} \end{align*}
    The base cases are:
    1. There is 1 way to tile a \(0 \times 2\) grid by 0 dominoes. There is nothing to prove.
    2. There is 1 way to tile a \(1 \times 2\) grid by 1 domino. Again, this is evident, and there is nothing to prove.
    For the induction step, let us suppose that, for some \(n \in \N\text{,}\) we already know that there are \(F_n\) tilings of the \((n-1) \times 2\) grid and \(F_{n+1}\) tilings of the \(n \times 2\) grid. Consider, then, a tiling of the \((n+1) \times 2\) grid. The rightmost column of this tiling has to look like one of the figures below.
    Now it is clear that each tiling that resembles the figure on the left is obtained from a unique \((n - 1) \times 2\) tiling by adding a single vertical domino. Similarly, each tiling that resembles the figure on the right is obtained from a unique \(n \times 2\) tiling by adding two horizontal dominoes. Since each tiling of the \((n+1) \times 2 \) grid is obtained from exactly one smaller tiling, we have shown (writing \(T_n\) for the number of tilings):
    \begin{align*} T_0 \amp = 1\\ T_1 \amp = 1\\ T_{n+1} \amp = T_{n-1} + T_{n}\text{.} \end{align*}
    It follows at once that \(T_n = F_{n+1}\text{,}\) since they obey the same recurrence relations.

    Activity 1.2.5.

    What is wrong with the following argument?
    Theorem 1.2.4. (Alleged theorem).
    All cars are blue.
    Proof.
    It is enough to show that given a nonempty finite set of cars, all of them are the same color. Since mine is blue, the result will follow at once. Let us prove by induction that every nonempty finite set of cars is monochromatic. If the set has just one car, it is surely monochromatic. Now let us suppose, by way of induction, that for some positive integer \(n\) it has already been shown that every set of \(n\) cars is monochromatic.
    Consider a set \(X\) of \(n + 1\) cars. We may form sets \(Y_1\text{,}\) \(Y_2, \ldots, Y_{n+1}\) by deleting the \(i\)th car from \(X\) to form \(Y_i\text{.}\) Applying the induction hypothesis to each of the sets \(Y_i\) we see that each of these sets is monochromatic. But then \(X\) is monochromatic as well, and the proof is complete.
